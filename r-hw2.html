    Problem 159. Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 7.

    Let \(a, b \in\Z\) with \(a\mid b\text{.}\) Prove that \(a^n\mid b^n\) for every positive integer \(n\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Let \(a, b \in\Z\) with \(a\mid b\text{.}\) Then there exists an integer \(k\) such that \(ak=b.\) By raising both sides of the equation to the \(n^{th}\) power, we get \(a^nk^n=b^n.\) Therefore, \(a^n\mid b^n\) for all nonnegative integers \(n.\)
    Rubric
    Does not demonstrate understanding.
    Contains a reasonable attempt to prove each part, but does not meet the criteria for two points.
    Needs revisions.
    Proof contains some significant gaps or excess information.
    Demonstrates understanding.
    Mathematically correct proof that there are only finitely many primes of the given form, with minor arithmetic, spelling, or grammatical errors. Or does not follow the Homework Guide for writing.
    Exemplary.
    Mathematically correct proof that there are only finitely many primes of the given form. The proof likely uses the hint from the back, showing the only possible prime of this form is \(p = 3.\) Work is easy to follow with formal mathematical writing.

    Problem 164. Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 25.

    1. Prove that all odd prime numbers can be expressed as the difference of square of two successive integers.
    2. Prove that no prime numbers can be expressed as the difference of two fourth power integers (Hint: Use the factorization tool discussed in the final paragraph of this section.)
    Solution.
    1. We will prove that all odd numbers can be written as the difference of squares of two successive integers, therefore all odd prime numbers can be written in this form. Let \(k\in\Z\) and consider \(2k+1\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{equation*} 2k+1= k^2+2k+1 - k^2 = (k+1)^2-k^2. \end{equation*}
      Since \(k,k+1\) are successive integers, we have the desired form.
    2. Let \(m,n\in\Z\) such that \(m^4-n^4\gt 0\) We will show that \(m^4-n^4\) is either composite or \(1.\) First note that \(m^4-n^4=(m^2-n^2)(m^2+n^2),\) and \(m^2+n^2\gt 0\) implies \(m^2-n^2\gt 0.\)
      If \(m^2+n^2=1,\) then one of \(m,n=1\) and the other is \(0.\) Thus, \(m^2-n^2=\pm1.\) If \((m-n)(m+n)=1,\) then \(m=1,n=0\) and \(m^2+n^2=1.\) Therefore, if either factor is \(1,\) \(m^4-n^4=1\text{.}\)
      If neither \(m^2-n^2,m^2+n^2\) is \(1,\) then the product is not prime by definition. Therefore, no prime can be written as the difference if two integers to the fourth power.
    Rubric
    1. Does not demonstrate understanding..
      Contains a reasonable attempt to prove each part, but does not meet the criteria for two points.
    2. Needs revisions..
      Proof contains some significant gaps or excess information.
    3. Demonstrates understanding..
      Mathematically correct proof for one part with most of the proof for the other. May contain minor arithmetic, spelling, or grammatical errors. Or does not follow the Homework Guide for writing.
    4. Exemplary..
      Mathematically correct proof for both parts, likely using factoring and case analysis for part (b). Work is easy to follow with formal mathematical writing.

    Problem 165. Ernst Problem 2.50.

    Consider the following statement: If \(x\in\mathbb{Z}\) such that \(x^2\) is odd, then \(x\) is odd. The items below can be assembled to form a proof of this statement, but they are currently out of order. Put them in the proper order.
    Assume that \(x\) is an even integer.
    We will utilize a proof by contraposition.
    Thus, \(x^2\) is twice an integer.
    Since \(x=2k\text{,}\) we have that \(x^2 =(2k)^2 =4k^2\text{.}\)
    Since \(k\) is an integer, \(2k^2\) is also an integer.
    By the definition of even, there is an integer \(k\) such that \(x=2k\text{.}\)
    We have proved the contrapositive, and hence the desired statement is true.
    Assume \(x\in \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\)
    By the definition of even integer, \(x^2\) is an even integer.
    Notice that \(x^2 = 2(2k^2)\text{.}\)
    Rubric
    Does not demonstrate understanding.
    Contains a reasonable attempt to prove each part, but does not meet the criteria for two points.
    Exemplary.
    All steps are in the correct order
