Section 8.2 Reading for March 13: Primitive roots modulo \(p\)

The original scan on Moodle is a bit hard to read, especially as some parts are annotated and some parts are covered up--since I have not receieved the book from interlibrary loan to re-scan, I will transcribe (incorporating the annotations/references to our textbook and examples.)

Subsection 8.2.1 Definition of primitive root

If \(a\) is a nonzero element of \(\Z_7\) (ie, \(a\not\equiv 0\pmod{7}\)), then Fermat’s Little Theorem tells us that \(a^6\equiv 1\pmod{7}.\) This implies that the order of every element of \(\Z_7\) is at most \(6\text{.}\) At the beginning of this chapter (Table of exponents modulo \(7\)) we saw that \(\ord_7 3=6.\) We can also say that the element \(3\in\Z_7\) has order \(6.\)

Definition 104. Definition 10.3.1.

Let \(p\) be prime and let \(\overline{a}\in \Z_p\) with \(a\not\equiv 0\pmod{p}.\) Then \(\overline{a}\) is said to be a primitive root in \(\Z_p\) or primitive root modulo \(p\) if \(\ord_p a=p-1.\)

Remark 105.

Primitive roots may also be defined for \(\Z_n\) when \(n\) is composite. (See Paper 3 topics)

Example 106.

  1. Is \(\overline{2}\in\Z_7\) a primitive root?
  2. Is \(\overline{5}\in\Z_7\) a primitive root?
Solution.
Computing powers of \(2\) and \(5\) modulo \(7,\) we find
\begin{align*} 2^1 \amp \equiv 2\pmod{7} \amp 5^1\amp \equiv 5\pmod{7}\\ 2^2 \amp \equiv 4\pmod{7} \amp 5^2\amp \equiv 4\pmod{7}\\ 2^3 \amp \equiv 1\pmod{7} \amp 5^3\amp \equiv 6\pmod{7}\\ \amp \amp 5^4\amp \equiv 2\pmod{7}\\ \amp \amp 5^5\amp \equiv 3\pmod{7}\\ \amp \amp 5^6\amp \equiv 1\pmod{7} \end{align*}
  1. Thus, the order of \(\overline{2}\) is \(3,\) so \(\overline{2}\) is not a primitive root (in \(\Z_7\))
  2. We see that the order of \(\overline{5}\) is \(6;\) hence, \(\overline{5}\) is a primitive root (in \(\Z_7\))

Example 107.

Determine which elements of \(Z_{11}\) are primitive roots.
Solution.
From Table of exponents modulo \(11\), we determined the orders of all of the nonzero elements of \(\Z_{11}:\)
\begin{align*} \ord_{11} 1\amp =1 \\ \ord_{11} 10\amp =2 \\ \ord_{11} 3\amp =\ord_{11} 4=\ord_{11} 5=\ord_{11} 9=5\\ \ord_{11} 2\amp =\ord_{11} 6=\ord_{11} 7=\ord_{11} 8=10. \end{align*}
Hence, the primitive roots in \(\Z_{11}\) (ie, the primitive roots modulo \(11\)) are \(\overline{2},\overline{6},\overline{7},\) and \(\overline{8}.\)

Example 109.

Find a primitive root in \(\Z_{17},\) if one exists.
Solution.
For want of a better strategy, we will check the orders of the elements \(\overline{1},\overline{2},\overline{3},\dots,\) until we find an element of order 16.
\begin{align*} \amp 1^1 \equiv 1\pmod{17} \amp 2^1 \equiv 2\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^1 \equiv 3\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp 2^2 \equiv 4\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^2 \equiv 9\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp 2^3 \equiv 8\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^3 \equiv 10\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp 2^4 \equiv 16\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^4 \equiv 13\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp 2^5 \equiv 15\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^5 \equiv 5\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp 2^6 \equiv 13\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^6 \equiv 15\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp 2^7 \equiv 9\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^7 \equiv 11\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp 2^8 \equiv 1\pmod{17} \amp\amp 3^8 \equiv 16\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^9 \equiv 14\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^{10} \equiv 8\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^{11}\equiv 7\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^{12} \equiv 4\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^{13} \equiv 12\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^{14} \equiv 2\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^{15} \equiv 6\pmod{17}\\ \amp\amp\amp\amp 3^{16} \equiv 1\pmod{17} \end{align*}
From the left column, we see that \(1\) has order \(1.\)
From the center column, we see that \(2\) has order \(8.\)
From the right column, we see that the order of \(3\) equals \(16,\) and we conclude that \(3\) is a primitive root modulo \(17.\)
We did not actually need to compute all of those powers of \(3\) to determine that \(3\) is a primitive root. We know that \(\ord_{17}3\) must be a divisor of \(17-1=16,\) by Proposition 5.1. Once we have computed from \(3^1\) to \(3^8\) and not yet obtained an answer of \(1,\) we know \(\ord-{17}3\gt 8.\) Since the only divisor of \(16\) that is greater than \(8\) is 16 itself, we can then conclude that \(\ord_{17}3=16.\) (This observation is generalized in Exercise 7.)
In Example 109, we found \(\overline{3}\) is a primitive root of \(\Z_{17}\) (ie, \(3\) is a primitive root modulo \(17.\)) Looking again at the column of powers of \(\overline{3}\) from the example, observe that these powers cycle through all of the nonzero elements of \(\Z_{17}.\) That is, every nonzero element is equivalent to \(\overline{3}\) raised to some power. This is a general fact about primite roots, which we will now prove.

Proposition 110.

Let \(p\) be a prime and let \(a\in\Z_p\) be a primitive root. Then every nonzero element of \(\Z_p\) appears exactly once on the list
\begin{equation*} \overline{a}^0,\overline{a}^1,\dots,\overline{a}^{p-2} \pmod{p}. \end{equation*}

Proof.

First, we show that the elements in the list (8.1) are distinct. Suppose that
\begin{equation*} a^j\equiv a^k\pmod{p} \end{equation*}
with \(j\) and \(k\) in the range \(0,\dots,p-2.\) Since \(\ord_p a=p-1,\) we know that the two powers of \(a\) are equal if and only if the exponents are congruent modulo \(p-1,\) by Proposition 5.2. Hence,
\begin{equation*} j\equiv k\pmod{p-1}. \end{equation*}
But since \(j\) and \(k\) are restricted to the range \(0,\dots,p-2,\) it follows that \(j=k.\) THus, we have show the elements in list (8.1) are all distinct.
Note that since \(a\not\equiv 0\pmod{p},\) each element in the like (8.1) is a nonzero element modulo \(p.\) There are \(p-1\) elements in list (8.1), and we have just shown that these elements are distinct. Since there are exactly \(p-1\) nonzero residues modulo \(p,\) list (8.1) must include every nonzero residue modulo \(p.\)

Subsection 8.2.2 Existence of primitive roots in a prime modulus

In Example Example 109, we asked whether there exists a primitive root in \(\Z_{17}.\) To solve this, we did not have to search very vary. Although \(\overline{1}\) and \(\overline{2}\) are not primitive roots we found that \(\overline{3}\) is a primitive roots. If we looke for primitive roots modulo other primes, we find a similar situation. For example, modulo \(47,\) we find that \(5\) is a primitive root, and modulo \(293,\) we find that \(2\) is a primitive toot, In fact, every prime less that \(100\) has a primitive root that is less than or equal to \(7.\) Every prime less that \(1,000,000\) has a primitive root that is less than or equal to \(73.\)
The main result of this chapter is the Primitive Root Theorem, which states that for every prime \(p,\) there exists a primitive root modulo \(p.\) Although this is easy to state, priving it is quite tricky. So tricky, in fact, that the proof given by the great Leonhard Euler was incorrect. It was Lagrange who gave the first correct proof of the Primitive Root Theorem.

Theorem 112. Primitive Root Theorem.

Let \(p\) be prime. Then there exists a primitive root modulo \(p.\)
To prove the Primitive Root Theorem, we must first prove that in \(\Z_,\) there exists an element of order \(p-1.\) Our first step is to prove that there exists an element of order \(q^s,\) where \(q\) is prime and \(q^s\) divides \(p-1\text{.}\) (Numbers of the form \(q^s\) where \(q\) is prime and \(s\in\N\) are called prime powers.)

Lemma 113. Lemma 10.3.4.

Let \(p\) be a prime and let \(q^s\) be a prime power (where \(q\) is prime and \(s\geq 1\)). If \(q^s\mid p-1,\) then there exits an element of order \(q^s\) modulo \(p.\)
Before proving this lemma, let’s see a quick example.

Example 114.

Suppose that \(p=101.\) Then \(p-1=100=2^2(5^2),\) which is divisible by the following prime powers:
\begin{equation*} 2^1=2, 2^2=4,5^1=5, 5^2=25. \end{equation*}
Thus, Lemma 10.3.4 guarentees that we will find elements of orders \(2,4,5,\) and \(25\) modulo \(101.\)

Proof of Lemma 10.3.4.

Let \(p\) be a prime. Let \(q\) be prime and \(s\in\N\) such that \(q^s\mid p-1.\) Consider the following equations in \(\Z_p:\)
\begin{align} x^{q^s}\amp \equiv 1\pmod{p}\tag{8.2}\\ \text{ and } x^{q^{s-1}}\amp \equiv 1\pmod{p}.\tag{8.3} \end{align}
By Proposition 5.8, equation (8.2) has \(q^s\) solutions and equation (8.3) has \(q^{s-1}\) solutions. Since \(q^s \gt q^{s-1},\) there must be at least one \(a\in\Z\) that is a solution to (8.2) and not (8.3). That is,
\begin{align} a^{q^s}\amp \equiv 1\pmod{p}\tag{8.4}\\ \textnormal{ but } a^{q^{s-1}}\amp \not\equiv 1\pmod{p}.\tag{8.5} \end{align}

Claim 115.

\(a\) has order \(q^s.\)

Proof.

By equation (8.4), it follows (using Proposition 5.1) that \(\ord_p a\) divides \(q^s.\) Thus, the order of \(a\) modulo \(p\) is one of the numbers in the list
\begin{equation} 1,q,q^2,\dots,q^{s-1},q^s.\tag{8.6} \end{equation}
However, from (8.5), it follows (again using Proposition 5.1) that \(\ord_p a\) does not divide \(q^{s-1}.\) Since the only number in list (8.6) that does not divide \(q^{s-1}\) is \(q^s,\) we conclude that the order of \(a\) modulo \(p\) is \(q^s.\)
Thus, we have found an integer \(a\) with order \(q^s\) modulo \(p.\)
Suppose we want to know whether \(\Z_{101}\) has primitive root--that is, an element of order \(100.\) Lemma 10.3.4 tells us that there is an element \(\overline{a}\) of order \(4,\) and element \(b\) of order \(25\) (see Example Example 114). It turns out that the product \(\overline{ab}\) will have order \(4\cdot 25=100\) and hence, \(\overline{ab}\) is the primitive root we seek. This is guarenteed by the following lemma.

Lemma 116. Lemma 10.3.5.

Let \(n\in\N,\) and let \(\overline{a},\overline{b}\in\Z_n\) be reduced residues (see: reduced residue system modulo \(m\)). If \(\ord_n a\) and \(\ord_n b\) are relatively prime, then
\begin{equation*} \ord_n (ab)=(\ord_n a)(\ord_n b). \end{equation*}

Proof.

On Homework 6--Strayer Chapter 5, Exercise 5a.
In Example Example 114, we used Lemma 10.3.4 to determine that \(\Z_{101}\) has orders \(4\) and \(25.\) By Lemma 10.3.5, the product of these elements has order \(100,\) os it is a primitive root. We will use this same argument, which combines Lemma 10.3.4 and Lemma 10.3.5 to give a general proof of the Primitive Root Theorem.

Proof of Primitive Root Theorem.

Let \(p\) be prime.
If \(p=2,\) then \(1\) is a primitive root modulo \(p.\) Thus, we may assume \(p\gt 2.\)
Factor \(p-1\) into primes:
\begin{equation*} p-1=q_1^{a^1}q_1^{a_2}\cdots q_m^{a_m}. \end{equation*}
By Lemma 10.3.4, for each \(k=1,2,\dots, m,\) there exists an integer \({x_k}\) of order \(q_k^{a_k}\) modulo \(p.\) Let
\begin{equation*} x\equiv x_1 x_2\cdots x_m\pmod{p}. \end{equation*}
Applying Lemma 10.3.5 repeatedly (see Exercise 11), we find
\begin{align*} \ord_p x \amp =(\ord_p x_1)(\ord_p x_2)\cdots(\ord_p x_m)\\ \amp =q_1^{a^1}q_1^{a_2}\cdots q_m^{a_m}\\ \amp =p-1 \end{align*}
Hence, \(x\) is a primitive root modulo \(p.\)
We have now proved that \(\Z_p\) always has at least one primitive root, but as we have seen, \(\Z_p\) often has more than one primitive root. It turns out that once we know one primitive root in \(\Z_p,\) it is easy to find all primitive roots in \(\Z_p.\) The following lemma tells us how to do this.

Lemma 117. Lemma 10.3.6.

Let \(p\) be prime, and let \(a\in\Z\) be a primitive root modulo \(p.\) Then for any \(j\in\Z,\) \(a^j\) is a primitive root modulo \(p\) if and only if \(\gcd(j,p-1)=1.\)

Proof.

Let \(p\) be prime, and let \(a\in\Z\) be a primitive root modulo \(p.\)
By Proposition 5.4,
\begin{equation*} \ord_p(a^j)=\frac{p-1}{\gcd(j,p-1)}. \end{equation*}
Thus, \(\ord_p(a^j)=p-1\) if and only if \(\gcd(j,p-1)=1.\)

Subsection 8.2.3 Naomi’s Numerical Proof Preview: Theorem 10.3.7

Let’s see whether we can use this llemma to determine how many primitive roots there are modulo \(19.\) Let \(\overline{a}\in\Z_{19}\) be a primitive root (modulo \(19\)). Then the nonzero elements of \(\Z_{19}\) are
\begin{equation*} \overline{a}^0,\overline{a}^1,\overline{a}^2,\dots,\overline{a}^{17}. \end{equation*}
Lemma 10.3.6 tells us which of these elements are primitive roots modulo \(19\text{:}\) precisely those in which the exponent is relatively prime to \(18.\) Thus, the primitive roots modulo \(19\) are
\begin{equation*} \overline{a}^1,\overline{a}^5,\overline{a}^7,\overline{a}^{11},\overline{a}^{13},\overline{a}^{17}. \end{equation*}
We see then that the primitive roots modulo \(19\) correspond to the exponents \(1,5,7,11,13\) and \(17,\) which are the positive integers less than \(18\) which are relatively prime to \(18.\) The nunber of such integers is exactly \(\phi(18)=6.\) In general, we have the following theorem.

Theorem 118. Theorem 10.3.7.

Let \(p\) be prime. Then there are exactly \(\phi(p-1)\) primitive roots modulo \(p.\)

Proof.

Let \(a\in\Z\) be a primitive root modulo \(p.\) Then by , we know that the list of reduced residues modulo \(p\) comprises the elements
\begin{equation*} a^0,a^1,a^2,\dots,a^{p-2} \end{equation*}
By Lemma 10.3.6, an integers \(a^j\) is a primitive root modulo \(p\) if and only if \(\gcd(j,p-1)=1.\) Thus, the number of primitive roots equals the number of positive integers \(j\) that are less than \(p-1\) and relatively prime to \(p-1.\) By ,definition, this is exactly \(\phi(p-1).\)
