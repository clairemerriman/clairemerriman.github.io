Problem 146. Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 36.

  1. Do there exist integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that \(x + y = 100\) and \((x, y) = 8\text{?}\)
  2. Prove that there exist infinitely many pairs of integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that \(x + y = 87\) and \((x, y) = 3\text{.}\)
Rubric
Does not demonstrate understanding.
Contains proofs for at least one part, but does not reach the level of Needs Improvement.
Needs revisions.
Contains correct definition of divisibility, with major algebra errors, such as \((x,y)\neq 3\text{.}\) Or proof has some significant gaps or excess information.
Demonstrates understanding.
Most of the work for the proof is present and clearly presented, but some steps are missing. Or does not follow the homework guide for mathematical writing, including correctly selecting pages for each problem.
Exemplary.
Mathematically correct proof using the definition of divides. Follows the homework guide for mathematical writing.
Solution.
  1. If \(x + y = 100,\) then \((x,y)\mid 100\) by linear combination. Since \(8\nmid 100,\) there does not exist integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that \(x + y = 100\) and \((x, y) = 8\text{.}\)
  2. Let \(x\in\Z\) with \(29\nmid x\) Let \(y= 100-x\text{.}\) Then \(3\mid y\) by linear combination. Then \((x,y)=3\) since \(29\nmid x\text{.}\) Thus, there are infinitely many \(x,y\in\Z\) such that \(x + y = 87\) and \((x, y) = 3\text{.}\)

Problem 148. Strayer Chapter 1, Exercises 38-40.

  1. Strayer Chapter 1, Exercises 38.
    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be relatively prime integers. Prove that \((a+b,a-b)\) is either \(1\) or \(2.\)
    Proof.
    Let \((a+b,a-b)=d\) and note that \(d\mid (a+b)+(a-b)=2a\) and \(d\mid (a+b)-(a-b)=2b\) by linear combination. By Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 37, \((2a,2b)=2(a,b)=2,\) since \((a,b)=1\) by assumption. Since \(d\) is a common divisor of \(2a\) and \(2b,\) \(d\mid 2\) by Lemma 41. Thus, \(d=1,2.\)
  2. Srayer Chater 1, Exercise 39.
    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be relatively prime integers. Find all values of \((a+2b,2a+b)\)
    Solution.
    Let \((a+2b,2a+b)=d\) and note that \(d\mid -2(2a+b)+(a+2b)=-3a\) and \(d\mid (a+2b)-2(2a+b)=-3b\) by linear combination. By Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 37, \((-3a,-3b)=3(a,b)=3,\) since \((a,b)=1\) by assumption. Since \(d\) is a common divisor of \(3a\) and \(3b,\) \(d\mid 3\) by Lemma 41. Thus, \(d=1,3.\)
    Note that both \(d=1\) and \(d=3\) can occur. For example, when \(a=b=1,\) we have \(a+2b=a+2b=3,\) so \((a+2b,2a+b)=3.\) When \(a=1,\) and \(b=2\) we have \(a+2b=5\) and \(2a+b=4,\) so \((a+2b,2a+b)=1.\)
  3. Exercise 40.
    Let \(a,b\in\Z\) with \((a,4)=2\) and \((b,4)=2\text{.}\) Find \((a+b,4)\) and prove that your answer is correct.
    Solution.
    Let \(a,b\in\Z\) with \((a,4)=2\) and \((b,4)=2\text{.}\) Then \((a+b,4)=4.\)
    Proof.
    Since \((a,4)=2,\) there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(2k=a\) and \(2\nmid k,\) otherwise \((a,4)=4.\) Similarly, there exists \(m\in\Z\) such that \(2m=b\) and \(2\nmid m.\) Therefore, \(k,m\) are both even. Thus, \(a+b=2k+2m=2(k+m)\text{.}\) By Part d–Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 11, \(k+m\) is even, so \(4\mid 2(k+m).\) Thus, \((a+b,4)=4.\)

Problem 149. Homework 3, Problem 4.

Let \(a_1,\dots,a_n\in\Z\) with \(a_1\neq 0\) and let \(d=(a_1,\dots,a_n).\) Show that \(c\in\Z\) is a common divisor of \(a_1,\dots,a_n\) if and only if \(c\mid d.\)

Proof.

We proceed by induction.
Base case, \(n=2:\) By Lemma 40, if \(c\in\Z\) is a common divisor of \(a_1,a_2\) then \(c\mid d.\) By Lemma 41, if \(c\mid d,\) then \(c\in\Z\) is a common divisor of \(a_1\) and \(a_2.\)
Induction Hypothesis: Assume that for all \(2\leq k \lt n,\) \(c\in\Z\) is a common divisor of \(a_1,\dots,a_k\) if and only if \(c\mid (a_1,\dots,a_k).\)
(\(\Rightarrow\))
Let \(c\in\Z\) is a common divisor of \(a_1,\dots,a_k.\) Then by the induction hypothesis, \(c\mid (a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}).\) Thus by the base case, \(c\mid ((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}),a_k),\) and by Proposition 42, \((a_1,\dots,a_k)=((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}),a_k).\) Thus, \(c\mid d=(a_1,\dots,a_k)\)
(\(\Leftarrow\))
Let \(c\in\Z\) such that \(c\mid (a_1,\dots,a_k).\) By Proposition 42, \((a_1,\dots,a_k)=((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}),a_k).\) Thus by the base case, since \(c\mid ((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}),a_k),\) \(c\) is a common divisor of \((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1})\) and \(a_k.\) Then by the induction hypothesis, \(c\) is a commom divisor of \(a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}\) and thus a common divisor of \(a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1},a_k.\)
