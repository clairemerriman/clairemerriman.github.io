Problem 97. Chapter 2, Exercise 71.

Using Euler’s Generalization of Fermat’s Little Theorem and the Chinese Remainder Theorem
  1. Let \(n\) be an integer not divisible by \(3\text{.}\) Prove that \(n^7\equiv n\pmod{63}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Let \(n\) be an integer that is not divisible by \(3\text{.}\) By the Chinese Remainder Theorem,
    \begin{align*} x \amp \equiv n^7 \pmod{7}\\ x \amp \equiv n^7 \pmod{9} \end{align*}
    has a unique solution modulo \(63.\) By Corollary 2.15, \(n^7\equiv n\pmod{7}.\)
    Since \((n,9)=1\) and \(\phi(9)=6,\) Euler’s Generalization of Fermat’s Little Theorem says that \(n^6\equiv 1\pmod{9}.\) Multiplying both sides of the congruence by \(n\) gives \(n^7\equiv n\pmod{9}.\) Thus, \(7\mid n^7-n\) and \(9\mid n^7-n\) by definition. Since \((7,9)=1,\) \(63\mid n^7-n,\) so \(n^7\equiv n\pmod{63}.\)
  2. Let \(n\) be an integer divisible by \(9\text{.}\) Prove that \(n^7\equiv n \pmod{63}\text{.}\)
    Remark 98.
    Reviewing the proof of part a: Corollary 2.15 only requires the modulus is prime. Euler’s Generalization of Fermat’s Little Theorem does require \((n,m)=1,\) so you cannot use it for this problem, but \(n\equiv 0\pmod{9}.\)
