Theorem 95. Theorem 3.2.

Let \(m\) and \(n\) be positive integers where \((m,n)=1\text{.}\) Then \(\phi(mn)=\phi(m)\phi(n).\)

Proof.

First, we note that an integer \(a\) is relatively prime to \(mn\) if and only if it is relatively prime to \(m\) and \(n,\) since \(m\) and \(n\) (together) have the same prime divisors as \(mn\text{.}\)
We can partition the positive integers less than or equal to \(mn\) into
\(1 \) \(\equiv m + 1 \) \(\equiv 2m + 1 \) \(\equiv \cdots \) \(\equiv (n-1)m + 1\pmod{m}\)
\(2 \) \(\equiv m + 2 \) \(\equiv 2m + 2 \) \(\equiv \cdots \) \(\equiv(n-1)m + 2\pmod{m}\)
\(\vdots \) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots \) \(\) \(\vdots\)
\(m-1 \) \(\equiv 2m - 1 \) \(\equiv 3m -1 \) \(\equiv \cdots \) \(\equiv nm - 1\pmod{m}\)
\(m \) \(\equiv 2m \) \(\equiv 3m\) \(\equiv \cdots \) \(\equiv nm\pmod{m} \)
For any \(b\) in the range \(1,2,\dots,m\text{,}\) define \(s_b\) to be the number of integers \(a\) in the range \(1,2,\dots, mn\) such that \(a\equiv b \pmod{m}\) and \(\gcd(a,mn)=1\text{.}\) For each equivalence class \(b,\) \(\gcd(b,m)\mid km +b\) by linear combination. Thus, \(s_b={0}\) if \((b,m)\gt 1.\) If \(\gcd(b,m)=1\text{,}\) the arithmetic progression, \(\{b, m + b, 2m + b, \dots, (n-1)m+ b\}\) contains \(n\) elements. By Strayer Chapter 3, Exercise 11, the arithmetic progression is a complete residue system modulo \(n,\) so \(\phi(n)\) elements are relatively prime to \(n\) and thus \(mn.\)
Thus, can see that when \((b,m)=1\text{,}\) \(s_b=\phi(n)\) and when \((b,m)\gt 1\text{,}\) \(s_b={0}\text{.}\)
Since all of the positive integers less than or equal to \(mn\) is in exactly one of the congruence classes above and t he \(s_i\) count how many integers in each congruence class are relatively prime to \(mn\text{,}\) \(phi(mn)=s_0+s_1+\cdots +s_{m-1}.\)
Since \(\phi(m)\) of the \(s_i=\phi(n)\) and the rest are 0, \(\phi(mn)=s_0+s_1+\cdots +s_{m-1}=\phi(m)\phi(n).\)
