Theorem 144. Theorem 4.6.

Let \(p\) be an odd prime number. Then
\begin{equation*} \legendre{-1}{p}= \begin{cases} 1, \amp p\equiv 1\pmod{4}\\ -1, \amp p\equiv 3\pmod{4} \end{cases}. \end{equation*}

Proof.

Let \(p\) be an odd prime number. Then from Euler’s Criterion, \(\legendre{-1}{p}\equiv (-1)^{(p-1)/2}\pmod{p}.\) Since both values are \(\pm1,\) we can say \(\legendre{-1}{p}=(-1)^{(p-1)/2}.\)
If \(p\equiv 1\pmod{4},\) then there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(p=4k+1.\) Thus, \(\frac{p-1}{2}=2k\) and
\begin{equation*} \legendre{-1}{p}=(-1)^{(p-1)/2}=(-1)^{2k}=1. \end{equation*}
If \(p\equiv 3\pmod{4},\) then there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(p=4k+3.\) Thus, \(\frac{p-1}{2}=2k+1\) and
\begin{equation*} \legendre{-1}{p}=(-1)^{(p-1)/2}=(-1)^{2k+1}=-1. \end{equation*}
