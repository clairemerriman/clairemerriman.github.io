Proposition 42.

Let \(a_1,\dots,a_n\in\Z\) with \(a_1\neq 0\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} (a_1,\dots,a_n)=((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{n-1}),a_n). \end{equation*}

Proof.

Let \(k=2\text{.}\) The since \(((a_1,a_2))=(a_1,a_2)\) by the definition of greatest common divisor of one integer, \((a_1,a_2)=((a_1,a_2))\text{.}\) The \(k=3\) case is Lemma 39.
Assume that for all \(2\leq k\lt n\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} (a_1,\dots,a_k)=((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}),a_k). \end{equation*}
Let \(d=(a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k}),\) \(e=((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k}),a_{k+1})=(d,a_{k+1}),\) and \(f= (a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k},a_{k+1}).\) We will show that \(e\mid f\) and \(f\mid e\text{.}\) Since both \(e\) and \(f\) are positive, this will prove that \(e=f\text{.}\)
Note that \(e\mid (a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k})\) and \(e\mid a_{k+1}\) by definition. Since \((a_1,\dots,a_k)=((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1}),a_k)\) by the induction hypothesis, \(e\mid(a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1})\) and \(e\mid a_k\) by Lemma 41. Again, by the induction hypothesis, \((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-1})=((a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-2}),a_{k-1}),\) so \(e\mid a_{k-1}\) and \(e\mid (a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_{k-2})\) by Lemma 41. Repeat this process until we get \((a_1,a_2,a_3)=((a_1,a_2),a_3)\text{,}\) so \(e\mid a_3\) and \(e\mid (a_1,a_2)\) by Lemma 41. Thus \(e\mid a_1,a_2,\dots,a_{k+1}\) by repeated applications of Lemma 41. By the generalized version of the Lemma 40 on Homework 3, \(e\mid f.\)
To show that \(f\mid e,\) we note that \(f\mid a_1,a_2,\dots, a_k,a_{k+1}\) by definition. Then \(f\mid d\) by the generalized version of the Lemma 40 on Homework 3. Since \(e=(d,a_k),\) we have that \(f\mid e\) by Lemma 40.
