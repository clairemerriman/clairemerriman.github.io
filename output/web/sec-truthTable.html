Section 1.2 Friday, January 19: Logic, proof by contradiction, and biconditionals

Objectives

  • Prove facts about divisibility
  • Prove basic mathematical statements using definitions and direct proof
  • Use truth tables to understand compound propositions
  • Prove statements by contradiction
  • Use the greatest integer function

Reading Questions 1.2.1 Reading Questions

Read Ernst Chapter 1 1  and Section 2.1 2 . Also read Strayer Introduction and Section 1.1 through the proof of Proposition 1.2 (that is, pages 1-5).

Turn In Turn In

1. Ernst Problem 2.6.
For \(n,m\in\Z,\) how are the following mathematical expressions similar and how are they different? In particular, is each one a sentence or simply a noun?
  1. \(\displaystyle n\mid m\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \frac{m}{n}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \sfrac{m}{n}\)
Solution.
The first means `` \(n\) divides \(m\text{,}\)" which is a relationship between \(n\) and \(m\text{.}\) This is a sentence. The other two are nouns, that is, the rational number \(\frac{m}{n}\text{.}\)
2. Ernst Problem 2.8.
Let \(a,b,n,m\in\Z\text{.}\) Determine whether each of the following statements is true or false. If a statement is true, prove it. If a statement is false, provide a counterexample.
  1. If \(a\mid n\text{,}\) then \(a\mid mn\)
    Solution.
    Let \(a\mid n\text{.}\) Then by definition, there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(ak=n\text{.}\) Multiplying both sides of the equation by \(m\) gives \(a(km)=mn,\) so \(a\mid mn\) by definition.
  2. If \(6\) divides \(n,\) then \(2\) divides \(n\) and \(3\) divides \(n\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Let \(6\mid n\text{.}\) Then by definition, there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(6k=n\text{.}\) By factoring \(6\text{,}\) we see that \(2(3k)=3(2k)=n,\) so \(2\mid n\) and \(3\mid n\text{.}\)
  3. If \(ab\) divides \(n,\) then \(a\) divides \(n\) and \(b\) divides \(n\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Let \(ab\mid n\text{.}\) Then by definition, there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(abk=n\text{.}\) Thus, we see that \(a(bk)=b(ak)=n,\) so \(a\mid n\) and \(b\mid n\text{.}\)
3. Ernst Problem 2.12.
Determine whether the converse of each of Corollary 2.9, Theorem 2.10, and Theorem 2.11 is true. That is, for \(a,n,m\in\Z\text{,}\) determine whether each of the following statements is true or false. If a statement is true, prove it. If a statement is false, provide a counterexample.
  1. If \(a\) divides \(n^2\text{,}\) then \(a\) divides \(n\text{.}\) (Converse of Corollary 2.9)
    Solution.
    False; \(4\mid 4\) but \(4\nmid 2\text{.}\)
  2. If \(a\) divides \(-n\text{,}\) then \(a\) divides \(n\text{.}\) (Converse of Theorem 2.10)
    Solution.
    True. If \(a\mid -n\text{,}\) then by definition, there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(ak=-n\text{.}\) Multiplying both sides by \(-1\) gives \(-ak=a(-k)=n.\) Therefore, \(a\mid n\text{.}\)
  3. If \(a\) divides \(m+n\text{,}\) then \(a\) divides \(m\) and \(a\) divides \(n\text{.}\) (Converse of Theorem 2.11)
    Solution.
    False; \(3\mid 2+1\) but \(3\nmid 2\) and \(3\nmid 1\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.2.3 Logic, proof by contradiction, and biconditionals

In Class Activity 1.

We will begin by working through Ernst Section 2.2 3  through Example 2.21. Discuss Problem 2.17 as a class, and note that Problem 2.19 is on Homework 1.
(a) Ernst Problem 2.17.
Determine whether each of the following is a proposition. Explain your reasoning.
  1. All cars are red.
  2. Every person whose name begins with J has the name Joe.
  3. \(x^2=4\text{.}\)
  4. There exists a real number \(x\) such that \(x^2=4\text{.}\)
  5. For all real numbers \(x\text{,}\) \(x^2=4\text{.}\)
  6. \(\sqrt{2}\) is an irrational number.
  7. \(p\) is prime.
  8. Is it raining?
  9. It will rain tomorrow.
  10. Led Zeppelin is the best band of all time.
(b) Ernst Problem 2.19.
Let \(A\) represent “6 is an even integer” and \(B\) represent “4 divides 6.” Express each of the following compound propositions in an ordinary English sentence and then determine its truth value.
  1. \(\displaystyle A \wedge B\)
  2. \(\displaystyle A \vee B\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \neg A\)
  4. \(\displaystyle \neg B\)
  5. \(\displaystyle \neg (A \wedge B)\)
  6. \(\displaystyle \neg (A \vee B)\)
  7. \(\displaystyle A \implies B\)
(c)
Construct a truth table for \(A\Rightarrow B, \neg (A\Rightarrow B)\) and \(A\land \neg B\text{.}\)
Solution.
This is the basis for proof by contradiction. We assume both \(A\) and \(\neg B\text{.,}\) and proceed until we get a contradiction. That is, \(A\) and \(\neg B\) cannot both be true.

Definition 9.

Proof by contradiction. Let \(A\) and \(B\) be propositions. To prove \(A\) implies \(B\) by contradiction, first assume the \(B\) is false. Then work through logical steps until you conclude \(\neg A \land A\text{.}\)
First, let’s define a lemma. A lemma is a minor result whose sole purpose is to help in proving a theorem, although some famous named lemmas have become important results in their own right.

Definition 10. Greatest integer (floor) function.

,
Let \(x\in\R\text{.}\) The greatest integer function of \(x\text{.,}\) denoted \([x]\) or \(\lfloor x \rfloor\text{.,}\) is the greatest integer less than or equal to \(x\text{.}\)

Lemma 11. Strayer, Lemma 1.3.

Let \(x\in\R\text{.}\) Then \(x-1\lt [x]\leq x\text{.}\)

Proof.

By the definition of the greatest integer function, \([x]\leq x\text{.}\)
To prove that \(x-1\lt [x],\) we proceed by contradiction. Assume that \(x-1\geq [x]\) (the negation of \(x-1\lt [x]\)). Then, \(x\geq [x]+1\text{.}\) This contradicts the assumption that \([x]\) is the greatest integer less than or equal to \(x\text{.}\) Thus, \(x-1\lt [x]\text{.}\)
