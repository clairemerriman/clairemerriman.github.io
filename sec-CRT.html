    Subsection 6.1.2 Multiplicative inverses

    Corollary 64. Corollary 2.8.

    Let \(a,m\in\Z\) with \(m\gt 0.\) The linear congruence in one variable \(ax\equiv 1\pmod{m}\) has a solution if and only if \((a,m)=1\text{.}\) If \((a,m)=1,\) then the solution is unique modulo \(m\text{.}\)

    Definition 65. multiplicative inverse of \(a\) modulo \(m\).

    Let \(a,m\in\Z\) with \(m\gt 0\) and \((a,m)=1.\) We call the unique incongruent solution to \(ax\equiv 1\pmod m\) the multiplicative inverse of \(a\) modulo \(m\).

    Example 66.

    Examples of multiplicative inverses:
    • \(5(3)\equiv 1\pmod{7}\) so \(3\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(5\) modulo \(7\) and \(5\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(3\) modulo \(7\text{.}\)
    • \(9(5)\equiv 1\pmod{11}\) so \(5\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(9\) modulo \(11\) and \(9\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(5\) modulo \(11\text{.}\)
    • \(8(-4)\equiv 8(7)\equiv 1\pmod{11}\) so \(7\equiv -4\pmod{11}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(8\) modulo \(11\) and \(8\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(7\equiv -4\pmod{11}\) modulo \(11\text{.}\)
    • \(8(5)\equiv 1\pmod{13}\) so \(5\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(8\) modulo \(13\) and \(8\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(5\) modulo \(13\text{.}\)
    Example using multiplicative inverses:
    \begin{align*} 6!\amp \equiv 6\ast 5\ast 4\ast 3\ast 2\ast 1\pmod{7}\\ \amp \equiv 6\ast 5(3)\ast 4(2)\ast 1\pmod{7}\\ \amp \equiv 6 \pmod{7} \end{align*}

    In Class Activity 9.

    Find \(10!\pmod{11}\) and \(12!\pmod{13}.\) Is there a pattern?
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} 10! \amp \equiv 10\ast 9\ast 8\ast 7\ast 6\ast 5\ast 4\ast 3\ast 2\ast 1\pmod{11}\\ \amp \equiv 10\ast 9(5)\ast 8(7)\ast 6(2)\ast 4(3)\ast 1\pmod{11}\\ \amp \equiv 1\pmod{11} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} 12! \amp \equiv 12\ast 11\ast 10\ast 9\ast 8\ast 7\ast 6\ast 5\ast 4\ast 3\ast 2\ast 1\pmod{13}\\ \amp \equiv 12\ast 11(6) \ast 10(4)\ast 9(3)\ast 8(5)\ast 7(2) \ast 1\pmod{13}\\ \amp \equiv 1\pmod{13} \end{align*}
    For a prime \(p,\) \((p-1)!\equiv 1\pmod{p}\text{.}\)
    We do need the condition that \(p\) is prime. For example, \(3!\equiv 2\pmod{4},\) and \(8!\equiv 0\pmod{9}.\)

    Subsection 6.1.3 Simultaneous Linear congruences in one variable (30 min)

    Example 67.

    Consider the system of linear equations
    \begin{align*} x \amp \equiv 2 \pmod{5}\\ x \amp \equiv 3 \pmod{7}\\ x \amp \equiv 1 \pmod{8}. \end{align*}
    A slow way to find an integer \(x\) that satisfies all three congruences is to write out the congruence classes:
    \begin{gather*} 2, 2+5, 2+5(2), \boxed{2+5(3)}, \dots\\ 3, 3+7, \boxed{3+7(2)}, 3+7(3), \dots\\ 1, 1+8, 1+8(2), \boxed{1+8(3)}, \dots \end{gather*}
    and see what integers are on all three lists. In addition to being tedius, we this doesn’t help find all such integers. To find all such integers, define \(M=5(7)(8)=280,\) and \(M_1=\frac{M}{5}=7(8),M_2=\frac{M}{7}=5(8),M_3=\frac{M}{8}=5(7).\) Then each \(M_i\) is relatively prime to \(M\) by construction. Thus, by Corollary 2.8 the congruences
    \begin{align*} M_1x_1 \amp \equiv 1\pmod 5, \amp 7(8)x_1\amp \equiv x_1 \equiv 1\pmod 5\\ M_2x_2 \amp \equiv 1\pmod 7, \amp 5(8)x_2 \amp \equiv 5x_2 \equiv 1\pmod 7\\ M_3x_3 \amp \equiv 1\pmod 8, \amp 5(7)x_3\amp \equiv 3x_3\equiv 1\pmod 8 \end{align*}
    have solutions. Thus, \(x_1\equiv 1\pmod{5}, x_2\equiv 3\pmod{7},\) and \(x_3\equiv 3\pmod{8}.\) Note that
    \begin{align*} M_1x_1(2)\amp =56(1)(2)\equiv 2\pmod 5, \amp M_2\amp \equiv M_3\equiv 0\pmod{5}\\ M_2x_2(3)\amp =40(3)(3)\equiv 3\pmod 7, \amp M_1\amp \equiv M_3\equiv 0\pmod{7}\\ M_3x_3(1)\amp =35(3)(1)\equiv 1\pmod 8, \amp M_1\amp \equiv M_2\equiv 0\pmod{8} \end{align*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} x=M_1x_1(2)+M_2x_2(3)+M_3x_3(1)=56(1)(2)+40(3)(3)+35(3)(1) \end{equation*}
    is a solution to all three congruences.

    Theorem 68. Chinese Remainder Theorem.

    Let \(m_1,m_2,\dots m_k\) be pairwise relatively prime positive integers (that is, any pair \(\gcd(m_i,m_j)=1\) when \(i\neq j\)). Let \(b_1, b_2,\dots, b_k\) be integers. Then the system of congruences
    \begin{align*} x\amp \equiv b_1 \pmod{m_1}\\ x\amp \equiv b_2 \pmod{m_2}\\ \amp \vdots\\ x\amp \equiv b_n \pmod{m_k} \end{align*}
    has a unique solution modulo \(M=m_1m_2\dots m_k\text{.}\) This solution has the form
    \begin{equation*} x=M_1x_1b_1+M_2x_2b_2+\cdot+M_kx_kb_k, \end{equation*}
    where \(M_i=\frac{M}{m_i}\) and \(M_i x_i\equiv 1 \pmod{m_i}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(m_1,m_2,\dots m_k\) be pairwise relatively prime positive integers. We start by constructing a solution modulo \(M=m_1m_2\dots m_k\text{.}\) By construction, \(M_i=\frac{M}{m_i}\) is an integer. Since each the \(m_i\) are pairwise relatively prime, \(\left(M_i, m_i\right)=1\text{.}\) Thus, by Corollary 2.8, for each \(i\) there is an integer \(x_i\) where \(M_i x_i\equiv 1 \pmod{m_i}\text{.}\) Thus \(M_i x_i b_i\equiv b_i\pmod{m_i}\text{.}\) We also have that \((M_i, m_j)=m_j\) when \(i\neq j\text{,}\) so \(M_i b_i\equiv 0 \pmod{m_j}\) when \(i\neq j\text{.}\) Let
    \begin{equation*} x=M_1x_1b_1+M_2x_2b_2+\cdot+M_kx_kb_k. \end{equation*}
    Then \(x\equiv M_i x_i b_i\equiv b_i\pmod{m_i}\) for each \(i=1,2,\dots,k\) and \(x\equiv M_i x_i b_i\equiv 0\pmod{m_j}\) when \(i\neq j.\) Thus, we have found a solution to the system of equivalences.
    To show the solution is unique modulo \(M,\) consider two solutions \(x_1,x_2.\) Then \(x_1\equiv x_2\pmod{m_i}\) for each \(i=1,2,\dots,k.\) Thus \(m_i\mid x_2-x_1\text{.}\) Since \((m_i,m_j)=1\) when \(i\neq j,\) \(M=[m_1,m_2,\dots,m_k]\) and \(M\mid x_2-x_1.\) Thus, \(x_1\equiv x_2\pmod M.\)
