    Problem 134. Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 5.

    Prove or disprove the following statements.
    1. If \(a,b,c,\) and \(d\) are integers such that \(a\mid b\) and \(c\mid d\text{,}\) then \(a+c\mid b+d\text{.}\)
    2. If \(a,b,c,\) and \(d\) are integers such that \(a\mid b\) and \(c\mid d\text{,}\) then \(ac\mid bd\text{.}\)
    3. If \(a,b,\) and \(c\) are integers such that \(a\nmid b\) and \(b\nmid c\text{,}\) then \(a\nmid c\text{.}\)enumerate
    Solution.
    1. False: Let \(a=b=c=1\) and \(d=2\text{.}\) Then \(a+b=2\) and \(c+d=3\text{.}\) Therefore, \(a+b\nmid c+d\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(a,b,c,\) and \(d\) be integers such that \(a\mid b\) and \(c\mid d\text{.}\) Then there exists \(m,n\in\Z\) such that \(a=bm\) and \(c=dn\text{,}\) by definition of divides. Multiplying these two equations gives \[ac=(bm)(dn)=(bd)(mn).\] Therefore, \(ac\mid bd\text{.}\)
    3. False: Let \(a=2, b=3,\) and \(c=4\text{.}\) Then \(a\nmid b, b\nmid c\) and \(a\mid c\text{.}\)enumerate
    Rubric
    Does not demonstrate understanding.
    Contains a reasonable attempt to prove each part, but does not meet the criteria for two points.
    Needs revisions.
    Two parts are mathematically correct, with the third containing major mathematical errors. Or proof has some significant gaps or excess information.
    Demonstrates understanding.
    Two parts are mathematically correct, with minor errors in the third part. Or does not follow the homework guide for mathematical writing.
    Exemplary.
    All three parts are mathematically correct and follows the homework guide for mathematical writing.

    Problem 135. Strayer Chapter 1, Exercise 10.

    1. Let \(n\in\Z\text{.}\) Prove that \(3\mid n^3-n\text{.}\)
    2. Let \(n\in\Z\text{.}\) Prove that \(5\mid n^5-n\text{.}\)
    3. Let \(n\in\Z\text{.}\) Is it true that \(4\mid n^4-n\text{?}\) Provide a proof or counter example.enumerate

    Proof.

    There are several ways to do these problems. I used slightly different methods for parts (a) and (b).
    1. Let \(n\in\Z\text{.}\) Then \(n^3-n=\text{.}\) By the Division Algorithm, there exists unique \(q,r\in\Z\) such that \(n=3q+r\) and \(0\leq r\lt3\text{.}\) Thus, \(n^3-n=27q^3+27q^2r+9qr^2+r^3-3q-r\)
      If \(r=0\text{,}\) then \(n^3-n=27q^3-3q=3(9q^3-q)\text{,}\) so\(3\mid n^3-n.\)
      If \(r=1\text{,}\) then \(n^3-n=27q^3+27q^2+6q=3(9q^3+9q^2+2q)\text{,}\) so\(3\mid n^3-n.\)
      If \(r=2\text{,}\) then \(n^3-n=27q^3+54q^2+15q+6=3(9q^3+18q^2+5q+2)\text{,}\) so\(3\mid n^3-n.\)
      Therefore, \(3\mid n^3-n\) in all possible cases.
    2. Let \(n\in\Z\text{.}\) Then \(n^5-n=n(n-1)(n+1)(n^2+1)\text{.}\) By the Division Algorithm, there exists unique \(q,r\in\Z\) such that \(n=5q+r\) and \(0\leq r\lt5\text{.}\)
      If \(r=0\text{,}\) then \(5\mid n.\) If \(r=1\text{,}\) then \(5\mid (n-1)=5q\text{.}\) If \(r=4\text{,}\) then \(5\mid (n+1)=5q\text{.}\)
      If \(r=2\text{,}\) then \(n=5q+2\) and \(n^2+1=25q^2+20q+4+1=5(5q^2+4q+1)\text{.}\) Therefore, \(5\mid (n^2+1)\text{.}\) If \(r=3\text{,}\) then \(n=5q+3\) and \(n^2+1=25q^2+30q+9+1=5(5q^2+6q+2)\text{.}\) Therefore, \(5\mid (n^2+1)\text{.}\) Thus, by transitivity of division, \(5\mid n^5-n\text{.}\)
    3. False: Let \(n=2\text{.}\) Then \(n^4-n=14\) and \(4\nmid 14\text{.}\)
    Rubrics:
    The hint in the back of the book says to use induction for part (a). That is not necessary and likely harder.
    Does not demonstrate understanding.
    Contains a reasonable attempt to prove each part, but does not meet the criteria for two points.
    Needs revisions.
    Two parts are mathematically correct with some missing details, with the third containing major mathematical errors. Or proof has some significant gaps or excess information.
    Demonstrates understanding.
    Two parts are mathematically correct, with minor errors. Or does not follow the homework guide for mathematical writing.
    Exemplary.
    All three parts are mathematically correct and follows the homework guide for mathematical writing.

    Problem 137. Ernst Problem 2.19.

    Let \(A\) represent ``6 is an even integer” and \(B\) represent ``4 divides 6.” Express each of the following compound propositions in an ordinary English sentence and then determine its truth value.
    1. \(\displaystyle A\land B\)
    2. \(\displaystyle A\lor B\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \neg A\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \neg B\)
    5. \(\displaystyle \neg (A\land B)\)
    6. \(\displaystyle \neg(A\lor B)\)
    7. \(\displaystyle A\Rightarrow B\)
    Solution.
    1. ``6 is an even integer and 4 divides 6". This is false because 4 does not divide 6.
    2. ``6 is an even integer or 4 divides 6". This is true because 6 is an even.
    3. ``6 is not an even number". This is false.
    4. ``6 is not an even number or 4 does not divide 6". This is true because 4 does not divide 6.
    5. ``6 is not an even number and 4 does not divide 6". This is false because 6 is an even number.
    6. ``If 6 is an even integer, then 4 divides 6". This is false because 4 does not divide 6.
    Rubrics:
    Does not demonstrate understanding.
    Contains a reasonable attempt to prove each part, but does not meet the criteria for two points.
    Needs revisions.
    Three or four parts are mathematically and grammatically correct sentences.
    Demonstrates understanding.
    Five or six parts are mathematically and grammatically correct sentences.
    Exemplary.
    All parts are mathematically and grammatically correct sentences.

    Problem 138. Ernst Problem 2.37.

    Let \(A\) and \(B\) represent the statements from Problem 2.19. Express each of the following in an ordinary English sentence.
    1. The converse of \(A\Rightarrow B\)
    2. The contrapositive of \(A\Rightarrow B\)
    Solution.
    Let \(A\) represent ``6 is an even integer” and \(B\) represent ``4 divides 6.”
    1. ``If 4 divides 6, then 6 is an even integer.`` Since 4 does not divide 6, any conclusion is vacuously true.
    2. ``If 4 does not divides 6, then 6 is not an even integer.`` Since 6=2(3), this statement is false.
    Rubrics:

    Both parts correctly translate the statements into grammaticallly correct sentences with a mathematically correct proof or counterexample.

    Problem 139.

    For each of the following equation, find what real numbers \(x\) make the statement true. Prove your statement.
    1. \(\displaystyle \lfloor x \rfloor + \lfloor x \rfloor =\lfloor 2x\rfloor\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \lfloor x + 3 \rfloor = 3 +\lfloor x\rfloor\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \lfloor x +3 \rfloor = 3 + x\)
    Solution.
    All of these statements could be stated as ``if and only if." The proofs below also work for both directions of the biconditional.
    1. If the decimal part of \(x\) is less than 0.5, then \(\lfloor x \rfloor + \lfloor x \rfloor =\lfloor 2x\rfloor\) . In otherwords, if \(x-\frac{1}{2} \lt \lfloor x \rfloor\leq x\text{,}\) then \(\lfloor x \rfloor + \lfloor x \rfloor =\lfloor 2x\rfloor\) .

      Proof.

      We will use the characterization \(x-\frac{1}{2} \lt \lfloor x \rfloor\leq x\text{.}\) Then we add this inequality to itself to find
      \begin{align*} x-\frac{1}{2}+x-\frac{1}{2} \amp \lt \lfloor x \rfloor +\lfloor x \rfloor \leq x + x\\ 2x-1 \amp \lt \lfloor x \rfloor +\lfloor x \rfloor \leq 2x. \end{align*}
      By Strayer, Lemma 1.3, \(2x-1 \lt \lfloor 2x \rfloor \leq 2x.\) Since the characterizations are the same, it must be that \(\lfloor x \rfloor +\lfloor x \rfloor=\lfloor 2x \rfloor .\)
    2. If \(x\in\R\text{,}\) then \(\lfloor x + 3 \rfloor = 3 +\lfloor x\rfloor\text{.}\)

      Proof.

      By Strayer, Lemma 1.3, for all \(x\in\R \text{,}\) \(x-1 +3 \lt 3 + \lfloor x \rfloor \leq x + 3\) and \(x + 2 \lt \lfloor x + 3 \rfloor \leq x +3 \text{.}\) That is, \(3 + \lfloor x \rfloor= \lfloor x +3 \rfloor\text{.}\)
    3. If \(x\in\Z\text{,}\) then \(\lfloor x +3 \rfloor = 3 + x\)

      Proof.

      Since \(\lfloor x +3 \rfloor \in \Z,\) \(\lfloor x +3 \rfloor = 3 + x\) requires \(x\in\Z\text{.}\) By part (b), \(\lfloor x + 3 \rfloor = 3 +\lfloor x\rfloor\) for all \(x\in\Z\text{.}\)

    Contains a reasonable attempt to prove each part, but does not meet the criteria for two points.
    Needs revisions.
    Two parts are mathematically correct, with the third containing major mathematical errors. Or proof has some significant gaps or excess information.
    Demonstrates understanding.
    Two parts are mathematically correct, with minor errors in the third part. Or does not follow the homework guide for mathematical writing.
    Exemplary.
    All three parts are mathematically correct and follows the homework guide for mathematical writing.
