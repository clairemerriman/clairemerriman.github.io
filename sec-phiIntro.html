Section 7.2 Wednesday, February 28: Modular arithmetic calculations and the \(\phi-\) function

Objectives

  • Use Euler’s Theorem to find the least nonnegative residue modulo a composite
  • Use Euler’s Theorem to find the multiplicative inverse of an integer modulo \(m\)
  • Prove \(\phi(4)\phi(5)=\phi(20)\) using an outline that mirrors the proof that \(\phi(m)\phi(n)=\phi(mn)\) when \((m,n)=1\)

Subsection 7.2.1 Multiplicative inverses using Euler’s Extension of Fermat’s Little Theorem (30 min)

Example 91.

  1. Find the least nonnegative residue of \(29^{202}\) modulo \(20\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The integers \(1,3,7,9,11,13,17,19\) are relatively prime to \(20.\) Thus \(\phi(20)=8.\) Also note that \(29\equiv 9\pmod{20}\) and \(202=8(25)+2,\) so
    \begin{equation*} 29^{202}\equiv 9^{202} \equiv(9^8)^{25} 9^2 \equiv 1^{25} 9^2\equiv 1\pmod{20} \end{equation*}
  2. Find the least nonnegative residue of \(71^{71}\) modulo \(16\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The integers \(1,3,5,7,9,11,13,15\) are relatively prime to \(16.\) Thus \(\phi(16)=8.\) Also note that \(71\equiv 7\pmod{16}\) and \(71=8(8)+7,\) so
    \begin{equation*} 71^{71}\equiv 7^{71} \equiv(7^8)^{8} 7^7 \equiv 1^{8} 7^7\pmod{16} \end{equation*}
    Since \(7^8\equiv 7^7 7\equiv 1\pmod{16},\) \(7^7\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(7\) modulo \(16.\)
    Using the Euclidean algorithm,
    \begin{align*} 16 \amp mp = 7(2) + 2,\amp 2\amp =16+7(-2)\\ 7 \amp = 2(3) + 1, \amp 1\amp =7-2(3)=7-(16+7(-2))(3)=16(-3)+7(7) \end{align*}
    Thus, \(7(7)\equiv 1\pmod{16},\) and \(7^7\equiv 7\pmod{16}.\)

Corollary 92. Corollary 2.19.

Let \(a,m\in\Z\) with \(m\gt 0.\) If \((a,m)=1,\) then \(a^{\phi(m)-1}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(a\) modulo \(m\text{.}\)

Example 93.

Find all incongruent solutions to \(9x\equiv 21\pmod{25}\text{.}\)
Solution.
The only positive integers less than \(25\) that are not relatively prime to \(25\) are \(5,10,15,20\text{.}\) Thus, \(\phi(25)=24-4=20.\)
Since \((9,25)=1,\) there is only one incongruent solution modulo \(25.\) By Corollary 2.19, \(9^{19}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(9\) modulo \({25}\text{.}\) Thus, \(x\equiv 21(9^{19})\pmod{25.}\)
Alternately, \(3^{18}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(3^2\) modulo \({25},\) so \(x\equiv 21(3^{18})\equiv (3^{19})7\pmod{25}.\)
The previous example does not ask for the least nonnegative residue, but let’s find it anyway.

Example 94.

Find the least nonnegative residue of \((9^{19})21\) modulo \(25\text{.}\)
Solution.
First, note that \((9^{19})21=(3^2)^19(21)\text{.}\) From here there are two options:
Factor \(21\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} (9^{19})21\equiv (3^2)^19(3)(7) \equiv (3^{39})(7) \equiv (3^{20})(3^{19})(7) \pmod{25} \end{equation*}
By Euler’s Generalization of Fermat’s Little Theorem, \(3^{20}\equiv 1\pmod{25}\) and by Corollary 2.19, \(3^{19}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(3\) moddulo \(25.\) Since \(3(-8)\equiv -24\equiv 1\pmod{25},\) \(3^{19}\equiv -8\pmod{25.}\) Thus,
\begin{equation*} (9^{19})21\equiv (-8)(7)\equiv -56\equiv 19 \pmod{25}. \end{equation*}
Using \(21\equiv -4\pmod{25}\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} (9^{19})21\equiv (3^2)^19(-4) \equiv (3^{38})(-4) \equiv (3^{20})(3^{18})(-4) \pmod{25} \end{equation*}
Since \(3^{20}=3^{18}(3^2)\equiv 1\pmod{25}\) by Euler’s Generalization of Fermat’s Little Theorem, \(3^{18}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(3^2=9\) modulo \(25.\) Since \(9(-11)\equiv -99\equiv 1\pmod{25},\) we have \(3^{18}\equiv -11\pmod{25}.\) Thus,
\begin{equation*} (9^{19})21\equiv (-11)(-4)\equiv 44 \equiv 19 \pmod{25}. \end{equation*}
