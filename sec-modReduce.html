    Section 7.1 Monday, February 26: Modular arithmetic calculations

    Objectives

    • Use Wilson’s Theorem to find the least nonnegative residue modulo a prime
    • Use Fermat’s Little Theorem to find the least nonnegative residue modulo a prime

    Subsection 7.1.1 Multiplicative inverses using Wilson’s Theorem

    Example 84.

    1. Let \(n\) be an odd positive integer. Then
      \begin{equation*} 2\left(\frac{n+1}{2}\right)=n+1\equiv 1\pmod{n}. \end{equation*}
      So \(\frac{n+1}{2}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(2\) modulo \(n\text{.}\)
      In Class Activity 11.
      Why is \(\left(\frac{n+1}{2},n\right)=1\text{?}\)
      Solution.
      Any common divisor of \(\frac{n+1}{2}\) and \(n\) also divides \(2\frac{n+1}{2}-n=1\) by linear combination.
      We also have that \(n-\frac{n+1}{2}= \frac{n-1}{2}\) and \(n-2\equiv -2\pmod{n},\) so \(\frac{n-1}{2}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(n-2\) modulo \(n.\) Another way to see this is
      \begin{equation*} -2\left(\frac{n-1}{2}\right)=-n+1\equiv 1\pmod{n}. \end{equation*}
    2. Let \(m\) and \(n\) be positive integers such that \(n\equiv 1\pmod m.\) Then there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(n=mk+1\) by definition. Then
      \begin{equation*} -m\left(\frac{n-1}{m}\right)=-n+1\equiv 1\pmod{n}. \end{equation*}
    3. Let \(m\) and \(n\) be positive integers such that \(n\equiv -1\pmod m.\) Then there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(n=mk-1\) by definition. Then
      \begin{equation*} m\left(\frac{n+1}{m}\right)=n+1\equiv 1\pmod{n}. \end{equation*}

    Example 85.

    1. Practice with Wilson’s Theorem: Find \(\frac{31!}{23!} \pmod{11}.\)
      \begin{align*} x\equiv \frac{31!}{23!} \amp \equiv 24(25)(26)(27)(28)(29)(30)(31) \pmod{11}\\ \amp \equiv 2(3)(4)(5)(6)(7)(8)(9) \pmod{11}. \end{align*}
      Then \(-x\equiv 10! \equiv -1\pmod{11}.\) Therefore, \(x\equiv 1\pmod{11}.\)
    2. Let \(p\) be an odd prime \(p,\) then \(2(p-3)!\equiv -1 \pmod{p}\text{.}\)
      Proof.
      Let \(p\) be an odd prime, then \((p-1)!\equiv -1\pmod{p}\) by Wilson’s Theorem. Multiplying both sides of the congruence by \(-1\) gives \((p-2)!\equiv 1\pmod{p}.\) Since \((p-2)!=(p-3)!(p-2)\) by the definition of factorial, \(p-2\equiv -2\pmod{p}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \((p-3)!\) modulo \(p.\) Thus,
      \begin{align*} -2(p-3)!\amp \equiv 1\pmod{p}\\ 2(p-3)!\amp -1\pmod{p}. \end{align*}

    Subsection 7.1.2 Finding least nonnegative residue Fermat’s Little Theorem

    Example 86.

    1. Find the least nonnegative residue of \(29^{202}\) modulo \(13\text{.}\)
      First, note that \(29\equiv 3\pmod{13}\) and \(202=12(10)+82=12(10)+12(6)+10=12(16)+10.\) Thus,
      \begin{equation*} 29^{202}\equiv 3^{202}\equiv (3^{12})^{16} 3^{10} \equiv 1^{16} 3^{10}\pmod{13} \end{equation*}
      From here, we have two options:
      Keep reducing:
      For this problem, this is the easier method:
      \begin{equation*} 3^{10}\equiv (3^3)^3 3\equiv (27)^3 3\equiv 3\pmod{13}. \end{equation*}
      Find inverse:
      Note that \(3^{12}\equiv 1\pmod{13},\) so \(3^{10}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(3^2\equiv 9\pmod{13}.\) Since \(9(3)\equiv 1\pmod{13},\) \(3^{10}\equiv 1\pmod{13}.\)
    2. Find the least nonnegative residue of \(71^{71}\) modulo \(17\text{.}\)
      First, note that \(71\equiv 3\pmod{17}\) and \(71=8(8)+7.\) Thus,
      \begin{equation*} 71^{71}\equiv 3^{71}\equiv (3^{8})^{8} 3^{7}\equiv 1^{8} 3^{7}\pmod{17} \end{equation*}
      Then
      \begin{equation*} 3^7\equiv 3^{3}(3^3)(3)\equiv 10(10)(3)\equiv 10(-4)\equiv -6\equiv 11\pmod{17}. \end{equation*}

    Corollary 87. Corollary 2.14.

    Let \(p\) be a prime. If \(a\in\Z\) with \(p\nmid a,\) then \(a^{p-2}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(a\) modulo \(p\text{.}\)

    In Class Activity 12.

    Prove: Let \(p\) be a prime. If \(a,k\in\Z\) with \(p\nmid a\) and \(0\leq k\lt p,\) then \(a^{p-k}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(a^k\) modulo \(p\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Let \(p\) be a prime. If \(a\in\Z\) with \(p\nmid a,\) then by Fermat’s Little Theorem, \(a^{p-1}\equiv 1\pmod{p.}\) If \(k\in\Z\) with \(0\leq k\lt p,\) then \(a^{p-1}=a^{p-k}a^k.\) Thus, \(a^{p-k}a^k\equiv 1\pmod{p}.\)

    Example 88.

    Find all incongruent solutions to \(9x\equiv 21\pmod{23}\text{.}\)
    Solution 1.
    Since\((9,23)=1,\) there is only one incongruent solution modulo \(23.\) By Corollary 2.14, \(9^{21}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(9\) modulo \({23}\text{.}\) Thus, \(x\equiv 21(9^{21})\pmod{23.}\)
    Solution 2.
    Since\((9,23)=1,\) there is only one incongruent solution modulo \(23.\) Since \(3^{20}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(3^2\) modulo \({23}\) by In Class Activity 12, \(x\equiv 21(3^{20})\equiv (3^{21})7\pmod{23}.\) Since \(3^{21}\) is the multiplicative inverse of \(3\) modulo \(23,\) so \(3^{21}\equiv 8\pmod{23}.\) Thus, \(x\equiv 7(8)\equiv 10\pmod{23}.\)

    Example 89.

    Let \(p\) be prime and \(a,b\in\Z\) with \(p\nmid a\) and \(p\nmid b.\) Then \(a^p\equiv b^p\pmod{p}\) if and only if \(a\equiv b\pmod{p}\text{.}\)
    Proof.
    Let \(p\) be prime and \(a,b\in\Z\) with \(p\nmid a\) and \(p\nmid b.\)
    (\(\Leftarrow\)) If \(a\equiv b\pmod{p},\) then \(a^p\equiv b^p\pmod{p}\) by repeated applications of Proposition 2.4.
    (\(\Rightarrow\)) If \(a^p\equiv b^p\pmod{p},\) then by Fermat’s Little Theorem,
    \begin{equation*} a\equiv a^{p-1}a\equiv b^{p-1}b\equiv b\pmod p. \end{equation*}
    Warning 90.
    This statement is only true for primes. Since
    \begin{equation*} 1^2\equiv 3^2 \equiv 5^2 \equiv 7^2\pmod{8},\quad 2^2\equiv 6^2\pmod{8}, \end{equation*}
    implies
    \begin{equation*} 1^8\equiv 3^8 \equiv 5^8 \equiv 7^8\pmod{8},\quad 2^8\equiv 6^8\pmod{8}, \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} 2^2\equiv 6^2\pmod{8}, \end{equation*}
    implies
    \begin{equation*} 2^8\equiv 6^8\pmod{8}. \end{equation*}
